3D Design of the Optical Device
Design project

This project was developed during my Bachelor’s degree in Engineering Physics. It focused on the computer-aided modeling of two optical devices. The first device was a signal aligner-coupler for an optical fiber, which presented the challenge of achieving precise calibration once physically assembled. The system required a manual micrometric calibration mechanism to properly align the optical components and ensure accurate experimental results. The second device was a Michelson–Morley interferometer, designed with the requirement that one of its optical elements could move along the x- or y-axis, as well as include a tilt adjustment mechanism modeled in SolidWorks to allow fine angular alignment of the mirrors during operation.

Overview

This project focuses on the design and planning of two optical devices aimed at solving specific calibration challenges through precise manual adjustments. The main objective is to develop a Michelson interferometer capable of micrometric manual adjustment and controlled motion along the x- and y-axes, enabling accurate calibration and visualization of interference patterns. Additionally, a fiber alignment device was designed to meet similar precision requirements, incorporating a manual TILT calibration mechanism based on screw-based adjusters and a guiding ball joint, ensuring fine optical alignment and system stability.

Project Development and Results

A positioning system with a TILT mechanism was designed to allow precise manual calibration of optical components. The mount includes a ball joint, two springs, and two screw-based adjusters that provide controlled angular and linear motion. A Michelson–Morley interferometer was modeled based on existing commercial designs. A dovetail rail was incorporated to enable smooth displacement of one mirror along a single axis, allowing fine adjustment and calibration of the interference pattern. Additionally, a fiber alignment device was developed to accurately couple an optical signal into a fiber core. It uses a manual TILT calibration system with pressors and a ball joint to ensure stable and repeatable alignment. Both devices required precise dimensional planning and the integration of micrometric adjustment mechanisms to meet experimental requirements and achieve reliable optical performance.
